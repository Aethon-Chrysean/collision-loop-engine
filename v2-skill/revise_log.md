- The Problem:
	- Both skills share a common architectural pattern: a "Theoretical Grounding" section at the top that establishes the cognitive principles, followed by a "Workflow" section that specifies procedural steps. The two sections are largely disconnected in register and reference. Once the AI enters the Workflow, the theoretical principles are rarely invoked explicitly. This creates a risk that the theoretical orientation — which is the most distinctive and valuable part of the design — decays as the AI progresses through the phases.
	- The LLMs' U-shaped attention: strong attention to the beginning and end of input, weak attention to the middle. The Theoretical Grounding sits at the beginning; the later Workflow phases sit in the middle. By the time the AI reaches Phase 2 or Phase 3, the philosophical orientation from the Theoretical Grounding may have substantially weakened in its influence on the AI's reasoning.
	- The Compliance Gap: the AI may produce outputs that *look like* they were generated under the influence of the theoretical principles (because it has learned what such outputs look like from training data) without actually engaging the deeper cognitive operations those principles prescribe. This "false compliance" is especially likely when the theoretical principles are separated from the execution steps, because the AI can satisfy each procedural step on its own terms without checking whether the step is consistent with the governing philosophy.
- Optimization Direction:
	- Dissolve the boundary between theory and procedure. Retain the initial Theoretical Grounding section, while simultaneously distributing its core insights into the respective phases where they are operationally relevant. These core insights, now embedded within each phase, may either match the content in the introductory Theoretical Grounding or differ from it; they can be further streamlined to capture the essential core, or expanded to deepen understanding.
	- Concretely, this means restructuring each phase to include a brief philosophical anchor — not a repetition of the full theory, but a one- or two-sentence statement that re-activates the relevant principle at the point where it matters most. For example:
		- In the collision-knowledge-generator, Phase 1.2 ("Evaluate Bisociative Potential") currently lists evaluation criteria (Structural Analogy Score, Surface Dissimilarity Score, Principle Specificity) without re-invoking the *reason* these criteria exist. An embedded anchor might read: "These criteria operationalize Koestler's bisociation condition — genuine creative collision requires that the two domains share structural depth but differ on the surface. If two candidate domains score high on structural analogy but also look and sound similar, the collision will produce routine interdisciplinary transfer, not bisociation. Only the conjunction of structural depth and surface foreignness produces the cognitive tension that generates genuinely novel ideas." This is not new information — it is in the Theoretical Grounding — but placing it here forces the AI to re-attend to the principle at the moment of decision.
		- In the collision-idea-generator, Phase 1.3 ("Execute the Collision") is the most critical step in the entire system, and it already contains an instruction block that says "perceive the problem SIMULTANEOUSLY through all active knowledge sets." But the *reason* this is necessary — that sequential application is comparative analysis and that only simultaneous perception creates the cognitive interference pattern that Koestler identified — is stated in the Theoretical Grounding but not re-invoked here. An embedded anchor would make the distinction operationally vivid at the moment the AI must execute it.
